Research & Methodology
The science behind every output

Every formula and recommendation in Physio Plan is grounded in peer-reviewed research. Scroll to explore the methodology — from nutrition targets to training volume to the exact formulas behind every calculation.

Nutrition

Protein — Cut: 1.2g/lb  |  Bulk: 1.0g/lb

During a caloric deficit, protein needs rise to prevent lean mass loss. Research shows elevated intakes of 1.8–2.7 g/kg body weight during restriction preserve significantly more muscle than standard recommendations. Higher protein specifically during a cut blunts lean mass loss in trained individuals even under aggressive energy restriction.

↗ Helms et al. (2014) — Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab. Protein needs during caloric restriction in resistance-trained athletes: 1.8–2.7 g/kg recommended. ↗ Longland et al. (2016) — Am J Clin Nutr. Higher protein in an energy deficit promotes greater lean mass gain and fat mass loss. ↗ Rondanelli et al. (2022) — PMC. Lean mass sparing in resistance-trained athletes during caloric restriction: role of training volume and protein intake.
Dietary Fat — 0.4–0.5g/lb for Hormone Support

Low-fat diets significantly reduce total and free testosterone. A meta-analysis of 6 intervention studies found meaningful decreases in total testosterone, free testosterone, and DHT on low-fat vs high-fat diets. Cholesterol — supplied by dietary fat — is the direct precursor for steroid hormone synthesis.

↗ Whittaker & Wu (2021) — J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol. Low-fat diets and testosterone in men: systematic review and meta-analysis of 6 intervention studies.
Thermic Effect of Food — 25% / 7.5% / 1.5%

Digesting food costs calories — and the cost varies dramatically by macronutrient. Protein is the most metabolically expensive at 20–30% of its caloric content, followed by carbohydrates at 5–10%, and fat at just 0–3%. This is why high-protein diets have a meaningful metabolic advantage beyond their muscle-sparing effects.

↗ Westerterp (2004) — Nutrition & Metabolism. Diet-induced thermogenesis: protein 20–30%, carbohydrate 5–10%, fat 0–3% of caloric content. ↗ Gibbons et al. (2014) — PMC / Front. Physiol. Thermic effect of food and appetite: individual participant data meta-analysis confirming protein-TEF relationship.
Calorie Surplus — Scaled by Experience (200–400 cal)

Research establishes two clear principles: (1) a surplus above maintenance is necessary to maximize muscle gain — eating at maintenance in trained individuals produces less lean mass than a surplus, and (2) larger surpluses beyond a moderate threshold increase fat gain without producing proportionally more muscle. Together, these constrain the optimal surplus to a moderate range. The specific values of 200/300/400 cal by experience level are derived from these principles combined with the well-documented decline in muscle gain rates with training age — not from a single RCT testing those exact numbers.

What the research directly supports:

↗ Ribeiro et al. (2019) — PMC. Caloric surplus produced greater lean mass gain than maintenance in trained bodybuilders, but also significantly more fat deposition. ↗ Barakat et al. (2023) — PMC. Larger surpluses (15% vs 5% above maintenance) primarily increased fat gain, not muscle or strength, in resistance-trained individuals. ↗ Schoenfeld et al. (2017) — J Sports Sci. Dose-response relationship confirms diminishing returns in muscle gain with training age, supporting smaller surpluses for advanced lifters.
Bulk Partitioning — 70% Lean / 30% Fat

During a caloric surplus with resistance training, weight gained is split between lean mass and fat. A pilot study in trained bodybuilders found a caloric surplus produced significantly more lean mass gain than maintenance, but also more fat deposition — with lean mass comprising roughly 30% of total mass gained (0.5 kg lean vs 1.1 kg fat). This aligns with the broader literature: larger surpluses increase fat gain proportionally more than muscle gain, and a moderate surplus of ~200–400 cal/day optimizes the lean-to-fat ratio.

↗ Ribeiro et al. (2019) — PMC. Caloric surplus vs. maintenance in trained bodybuilders: greater lean mass but also greater fat deposition with surplus. ↗ Barakat et al. (2023) — PMC. Larger energy surpluses primarily increase fat gain rather than augmenting strength or muscle thickness in resistance-trained individuals.
Deficit Size — 300 / 500 / 750 cal & LBM Retention

A meta-analysis and meta-regression found that a deficit of ~500 kcal/day was sufficient to fully blunt lean mass gains during resistance training. Larger deficits proportionally increase lean mass losses — the basis for scaling expected muscle loss by deficit size and experience level.

↗ Murphy et al. (2021) — PubMed. Energy deficiency impairs resistance training gains in lean mass: meta-analysis showing ~500 kcal/day deficit blunts LBM gains.
Carbohydrates — Remaining Calories for Performance

After protein and fat minimums are met, remaining calories fill carbohydrates. Carbs are the primary fuel for glycolytic exercise and help maintain anabolic hormone levels — low carbohydrate availability during training has been associated with reduced testosterone and impaired workout performance.

↗ Nunes et al. (2022) — J Int Soc Sports Nutr. Protein intake to support muscle mass and function in resistance-trained adults: systematic review and meta-analysis.

Training

Frequency — 2x/week Per Muscle Group

A landmark meta-analysis established that training each muscle group at least twice per week produces superior hypertrophic outcomes compared to once per week. Training each muscle group at least twice per week is the standard recommendation.

↗ Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016) — Sports Medicine. Effects of resistance training frequency on measures of muscle hypertrophy: a systematic review and meta-analysis.
Volume — 6–22 Sets/Muscle/Week by Level

Hypertrophy follows a dose-response relationship with volume up to a recoverable ceiling. Israetel's volume landmark framework (MEV, MAV, MRV) and Schoenfeld's dose-response meta-analysis form the basis for the experience-differentiated volume ranges.

↗ Israetel & Krieger — Renaissance Periodization. Volume landmarks for hypertrophy: MEV, MAV, and MRV by muscle group and experience level. ↗ Schoenfeld et al. (2017) — J Sports Sci. Dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and increases in muscle mass.
Intensity — RPE 7–9.5 by Experience

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) provides a practical, individualized intensity metric. Beginners train further from failure to build motor patterns; advanced lifters train closer to failure to maintain the hypertrophic stimulus as the adaptive ceiling rises.

↗ Schoenfeld & Grgic (2019) — Strength & Cond J. Does training to failure maximize muscle hypertrophy? Review of the evidence.
Body Recomposition — Simultaneous Muscle & Fat Change

Despite conventional wisdom that muscle gain and fat loss can't happen simultaneously, a systematic review confirmed recomposition in resistance-trained individuals across multiple randomized controlled trials. Rate is highest in beginners and declines sharply with experience — the basis for the experience-differentiated monthly rates used here.

↗ Barakat et al. (2020) — Strength & Conditioning Journal. Body recomposition: can trained individuals build muscle and lose fat at the same time? Review of RCTs demonstrating recomposition across populations.

How It's Calculated

A plain-English walkthrough of every formula used when you hit Generate.

Step 1

How many calories does your body burn at rest?

Most calculators estimate this using total bodyweight — but that ignores body composition. A 180 lb person at 10% body fat burns significantly more calories at rest than a 180 lb person at 25% body fat, because muscle is metabolically active and fat is not. The Katch-McArdle formula corrects for this by using lean mass only.

The formula
Lean Mass (kg) = bodyweight × (1 − body fat%) × 0.454
Resting Calories = 370 + (21.6 × Lean Mass in kg)
Example: 180 lbs at 15% body fat → 153 lbs lean mass → ~68 kg → resting burn of ~1,838 calories/day
Step 2

How many calories do you burn moving around?

Your resting burn is just the baseline. On top of that, your body burns calories through everyday movement (walking, fidgeting, standing — this is called NEAT) and through actual workouts. We scale these separately so the estimate accounts for both.

The formula
Daily movement calories = Resting Calories × activity multiplier
Workout calories = (training days per week × 300) ÷ 7

Step 3

How many calories does digestion burn? (most calculators skip this)

Digesting food actually costs calories — your body has to break it down and process it. This is called the Thermic Effect of Food (TEF). Protein is the most expensive to digest, carbs moderate, and fat very cheap. Most TDEE calculators ignore this entirely. Because TEF depends on macros, which depend on total calories, which depend on TEF, this is solved iteratively — running the calculation in a loop until the numbers stabilize.

Step 4

What's your daily calorie target?

Once maintenance is known, your target is set based on your goal. Cutting means eating below maintenance to lose fat. Bulking means eating above it to build muscle — but the surplus is kept smaller for advanced lifters because their bodies are less efficient at converting extra calories into muscle. Recomp stays at maintenance.

Step 5

How are calories split into protein, fat, and carbs?

Protein and fat are assigned first as minimums based on bodyweight — protein to preserve or build muscle, fat to support hormone production. Every remaining calorie goes to carbohydrates to fuel workouts. Protein needs are set higher on a cut because the body is more likely to break down muscle when calories are restricted.

CUTTING / RECOMP
Protein: 1.2g per lb of bodyweight
Fat: 0.4g per lb of bodyweight
Carbs: whatever's left
BULKING
Protein: 1.0g per lb of bodyweight
Fat: 0.5g per lb of bodyweight
Carbs: whatever's left
Step 6

Where will that weight loss (or gain) come from?

Not all weight lost during a cut is fat — some is muscle. Not all weight gained during a bulk is muscle — some is fat. The model accounts for both directions based on experience level and deficit or surplus size.

CUTTING — How much of your weight loss is muscle vs. fat?

We estimate that a small portion of the weight you lose will be muscle (not just fat). That portion depends on how advanced you are and how aggressive your deficit is — larger deficits and more experienced lifters lose a higher proportion of muscle. The rest is fat loss.

Beginner: 0–10% of weight lost is muscle  |  Intermediate: 10–20%  |  Advanced: 15–30%
BULKING — How much of your weight gain is muscle vs. fat?

Research suggests roughly 60–80% of weight gained in a clean bulk is lean mass. 70% is used as a realistic middle estimate.

70% of weight gained → muscle  |  30% → fat
RECOMP — Gaining muscle and losing fat at the same time

Recomp is slow but real — especially for beginners. The body reallocates energy, building muscle from protein and training stimulus while slowly oxidizing fat stores. Rate slows significantly with experience.

Cutting to a target body fat % (not just a weight)

When a goal body fat percentage is entered instead of a goal weight, the calculation works backwards to find the correct target weight. Because weight loss includes some lean mass, the target weight must account for this — otherwise the final body fat percentage would be off. The estimate is refined iteratively until accurate to within 0.01%.

Step 7

How long will it take?

One pound of body fat contains roughly 3,500 calories. To lose that pound, you need to run a 3,500 calorie deficit over time. Your daily deficit determines how fast that happens. The total calorie gap is divided by the daily deficit to calculate how many weeks the process will take.

The formula
Weeks to goal = (pounds to lose × 3,500) ÷ (daily deficit × 7)
Example: Losing 20 lbs at a 500 cal/day deficit → (20 × 3,500) ÷ (500 × 7) = 20 weeks
